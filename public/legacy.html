CME

Coronal Mass Ejections are massive bursts of solar wind and magnetic fields rising above the solar corona or being released into space, capable of causing significant space weather effects.
Key characteristics of CMEs:

  • 1. Massive Plasma Ejection: CMEs release billions of tons of plasma and magnetic field into space at speeds up to 3,000 km/s.

  • 2. Magnetic Field Disruption: They carry twisted magnetic field lines that can interact with Earth's magnetosphere.

  • 3. Geomagnetic Impact: When directed toward Earth, CMEs can cause severe geomagnetic storms and aurora displays.

  • 4. Technology Effects: Can disrupt satellite operations, GPS systems, and power grids on Earth.

  • 5. Solar Cycle Correlation: CME frequency varies with the 11-year solar cycle, peaking during solar maximum.

Source: https://science.nasa.gov/astrophysics/focus-areas/how-do-stars-form-and-evolve/

Solar Flare

Intense bursts of electromagnetic radiation from the Sun's surface, releasing enormous amounts of energy that can affect Earth's atmosphere and technological systems.

Solar flares are sudden releases of electromagnetic energy from the Sun's corona, often associated with sunspot activity and magnetic field reconnection.

They are classified by intensity from A-class (smallest) to X-class (largest), with X-class flares capable of causing radio blackouts and satellite damage.

Solar flares can disrupt high-frequency radio communications, GPS navigation systems, and pose radiation hazards to astronauts and airline passengers.

The most powerful flares can trigger geomagnetic storms when their associated particles reach Earth's magnetosphere.

Source: https://www.nasa.gov/solar-flares/

Solar Storm

Major disturbances in space weather caused by solar activity, including solar flares and coronal mass ejections, that can significantly impact Earth's technological infrastructure.

Solar storms are complex space weather events that combine multiple solar phenomena, creating cascading effects throughout the solar system.

They can cause widespread power grid failures, satellite malfunctions, and complete disruption of radio communications across entire continents.

Solar storms pose significant risks to astronauts, airline crews, and passengers on polar routes due to increased radiation exposure.

Source: https://www.nasa.gov/solar-storms/

Aurora

Spectacular natural light displays in polar skies caused by charged particles from the solar wind interacting with Earth's magnetic field and atmosphere, creating breathtaking curtains of colored light.

Auroras occur when charged particles from the solar wind are guided by Earth's magnetic field lines into the upper atmosphere, where they collide with gas molecules and create luminous displays.

The colors of auroras depend on the type of gas molecules and altitude: oxygen produces green and red lights, while nitrogen creates blue and purple hues.

Aurora Borealis (Northern Lights) and Aurora Australis (Southern Lights) are most commonly visible in polar regions but can extend to lower latitudes during intense geomagnetic storms.

These magnificent displays have inspired countless myths and legends throughout human history and continue to attract millions of visitors to polar regions each year.

Source: https://www.nasa.gov/aurora/
